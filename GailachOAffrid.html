Gailach O'Affrid- A Witchfolk Nursery Rhyme



Harken ye, me bairns and wains,
To a tale made dark wit bludstains,
A tale ye myte ha' heared afore,
O' th' boye that lived 'pon Affrid's shore.


Th' yonge Gailach, borne of meagre kyn;
His family were but fishermen.
Yet Gailach yearned for somethin' more,
Thenne hooking fish from Affrid's shore.


Chetwig Woode, they lived hard-bye,
Soo one nyghte 'neath a moonful skye,
He fled to Chetwig, kyn foresworn,
And vowed na'er return to Affrid's shore.


Twixt gnarled bole and pustuled vine,
'Neath myty oake and nettled pine,
Th' yonge Gailach fled to his doom,
In Chetwig's greene bedevil'd gloom.


Ere dawn he came upon a glede,
Where bale-eyed beaste and gray wolf bayed.
Wyth offal were the boughs festooned,
In Chetwig's bleke bedevil'd gloom.


Man-shaped goats and three-eyed beeves,
Held coven 'neath the oaken leaves,
Warped beastes gambol'd 'neath the moon,
In Chetwig's dread bedevil'd gloom.


In their midst was ruin'd throne,
Made o' flesh and twig and bone.
'Pon it was a bloat'd drake;
Benyght'd mystress of the brake.


Gailach had no spere nor sworde,
To parry fange nor horn nor gore.
As they circled, th' boye quaked,
Afore the horrors of the brake.


The swollen serpent croak'd and rasp'd,
And belch'd forth a fetid gas.
Gailach, engulf'd, began to shake,
And then collaps'd within the brake.


Th' yonge Gailach crawl'd to the throne,
His bones and skin all warp'd and torn.
Changed by the fug, th' newe beast spake,
To his newe god, queen of the brake.


“Bid me, mother” spake he to her.
Perched 'pon her throne, the devil purred,
“Cavort with thy kyn 'twixt the blooms,
In Chetwig's feye bedevil'd gloom.”


One more beaste among bestial host,
Th' boye, changed, is forever lost.
And Gailach returned nevermore,
To his kyn 'pon the Affrid's shore
